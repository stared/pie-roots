# *sed- 'to sit' (full descent tree)

> Verification tier: **exploration** — chains follow Wiktionary, cross-checked
> against Etymonline; disputed or weakly-sourced links are marked `?`.
> Chart: https://p.migdal.pl/pie-roots/sed/

```
*sed- (PIE) 'to sit'
├── *nisdós (PIE!) 'nest' = *ni 'down' + *-sd- — a compound formed at PIE level, arrives in English TWICE
│   ├── PGmc *nestaz → OE nest → nest, nestle                       [by blood]
│   └── Latin nīdus 'nest'                                          [by borrowing]
│       ├── Old French niais 'nestling falcon' → eyas ("a nyas" rebracketed to "an eyas")
│       └── French nicher 'to nest' → ? niche (competing: Italian nicchia 'seashell')
├── Germanic arm
│   ├── *sitjaną → OE sittan → sit
│   ├── *satjaną (causative 'make sit') → OE settan → set
│   │   └── German setzen → ersetzen 'replace' → ersatz
│   ├── ? *setlaz → OE setl → settle  [sources disagree on attachment to *sed-]
│   ├── *sētiją → ON sæti → seat        [Norse loan, not native OE!]
│   ├── *sōtam 'what settles' → OE sōt → soot
│   ├── ? *sadulaz → saddle  [OED: formal difficulties]
│   └── OE cotsǣta 'cottage-sitter' → ? cosset (pet lamb → pamper)
├── Latin arm (the heaviest)
│   ├── sedēre 'to sit'
│   │   ├── [group] session, sedentary, sediment, sessile, séance
│   │   ├── [group] -sidēre compounds: preside, reside, subside, assiduous, insidious, dissident, supersede
│   │   ├── assidēre 'sit beside (as judge)' → OF assise 'court sitting' → assize → size; assess
│   │   ├── ? possidēre 'probably potis + sedēre' → possess; obsidēre 'sit against' → obsess
│   │   │   └── obses 'one who sits before, hostage' → ? hostage (competing hospit- derivation)
│   │   ├── VLat *sedicum → OF sege → siege, besiege
│   │   └── sēdāre 'settle, calm' → sedate, sedative
│   ├── sēdēs 'seat' → OF sie(d) → see (bishop's)
│   ├── solium 'throne' → ? soil (conflated with solum 'ground')
│   └── ? cēdō 'go, yield' (*ke-sd- 'sit away'?) → cede, concede, proceed, succeed…  [disputed]
├── Greek arm
│   ├── ἕδρα 'seat; face of a solid'
│   │   ├── [group] -hedron: polyhedron, tetrahedron, exedra
│   │   ├── καθέδρα 'a sitting-down' → Latin cathedra → cathedral, ex cathedra; → OF chaiere → chair; → chaise
│   │   ├── συνέδριον 'sitting-together, council' → Hebrew → Sanhedrin
│   │   └── ἐφέδρα 'sitting-upon (plant)' → ephedra, ephedrine
│   └── ? πιέζω 'press' (*pi-sed- 'sit upon') → piezo-  [Wiktionary prefers *peysd-]
├── Celtic arm
│   ├── Welsh eistedd 'to sit' → eisteddfod 'session' → Eisteddfod
│   └── *sīdos 'seat; fairy mound' → OIr síd → Irish bean sí 'woman of the mound' → banshee
├── Indo-Iranian arm: Skt upa-ni-ṣad 'a sitting-down beside' → Upanishad
└── Balto-Slavic arm [context only]: Lith sėdė́ti; Polish siedzieć; sad 'orchard' (causative 'what is set')
```

## Transition sources

## Additional English candidates (researched 2026-07-20; not in chart)

The published chart remains a subset. These were checked as possible additions,
with compounds grouped by the actual intermediate verb rather than attached
straight to `*sed-`.

### Strong and potentially surprising

- **assess**: Latin `assidēre` “sit beside” → `assessus` “a sitting by” →
  Medieval Latin `assessāre`, originally fixing a tax or fine as a judge's
  assistant → assess. This is especially strong: Wiktionary gives the complete
  etymology tree and Etymonline explains the legal semantic transition.
  https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/assess · https://www.etymonline.com/word/assess
- **insidious**: Latin `īnsidēre` “sit in or on” → `īnsidiae` “a lying in
  wait, ambush” → `īnsidiōsus` “cunning, treacherous” → insidious.
  https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/insidious · https://www.etymonline.com/word/insidious
- **obsess**: Latin `obsidēre`, literally “sit against,” meant “besiege”; the
  English word moved from besieging to haunting or tormenting, then to a fixed
  idea. This would echo the existing siege branch without duplicating it.
  https://www.etymonline.com/word/obsess
- **subsidy**: Latin `subsidere` “sit down; remain” → `subsidium`, originally
  troops held in reserve and then aid or support → subsidy. Both sources make
  the `sub- + sīdō/sedēre` analysis explicit.
  https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/subsidy · https://www.etymonline.com/word/subsidy
- **supersede**: Latin `supersedēre`, literally “sit above,” developed through
  “refrain/postpone” to “set aside, replace.” The spelling preserves the Latin
  stem unusually clearly.
  https://www.etymonline.com/word/supersede
- **dissident**: Latin `dissidēre`, literally “sit apart,” already meant
  disagree or be at variance.
  https://www.etymonline.com/word/dissident
- **assiduous**: Latin `assiduus` from `assidēre` “sit by”; continual presence
  became constant application to work.
  https://www.etymonline.com/word/assiduous
- **president / preside**: Latin `praesidēre`, literally “sit before,” meant
  guard, superintend, or act as head. Secure and culturally resonant, though
  the modern authority metaphor is less surprising than the candidates above.
  https://www.etymonline.com/word/preside
- **séance**: French `séance` was simply “a sitting, session” before English
  narrowed it to a spiritualist sitting.
  https://www.etymonline.com/word/seance
- **sediment**: Latin `sedimentum` was a settling or sinking down. Secure, but
  transparent beside soot and sedate.
  https://www.etymonline.com/word/sediment
- **session**: Latin `sessiō` was the act of sitting, then a court or assembly's
  sitting. Secure, but semantically unsurprising.
  https://www.etymonline.com/word/session
- **Somerset**: Old English `Sumorsǣton` named the people settled at Somerton;
  its final element is the old “residents, inhabitants” sense of `sæt`.
  https://www.etymonline.com/word/Somerset · https://www.etymonline.com/word/seat

### Plausible, but the chart would need a caveat

- **possess**: Latin `possidēre` is *probably* `potis` “powerful/master” +
  `sedēre` “sit,” originally a legal term associated with occupying real
  estate. Etymonline explicitly says “probably,” so this must not be drawn as
  certain.
  https://www.etymonline.com/word/possess
- **soil**: the English noun appears to merge three Old French words; only one,
  `soeul/sueil` “threshold, area, place,” comes from Latin `solium` “seat.” The
  verb may instead derive through a word for a pig. This is too conflated for a
  clean undashed endpoint.
  https://www.etymonline.com/word/soil
- **saddle**: often connected through Proto-Germanic `*sadulaz`, but the full
  research tree already records the OED's formal objections. Retain as
  disputed unless stronger specialist evidence resolves them.
- **settle**: Etymonline attaches it to `*sed-`, while current Wiktionary rejects
  that derivation on phonological grounds; see the transition note above.
- **cosset**: the proposed route runs through Old English `cot-sæta`, a
  “cot-dweller,” to a pet lamb and then “pamper,” but Etymonline calls the
  origin uncertain and attributes this only as Skeat's proposal.
  https://www.etymonline.com/word/cosset
- **nick** “notch” may be a variant of French `niche`, whose own `nīdus`
  ancestry is disputed. Two uncertainties in series make it unsuitable for the
  chart.
  https://www.etymonline.com/word/niche

### Tempting lists that should not be presented as established

- **method, period, exodus, synod, cathode/anode and other Greek `-hodos/-ode`
  words** depend on deriving Greek `ὁδός` “way, path” from a `*sod-` formation
  connected with `*sed-`. Wiktionary currently includes that route, but
  Etymonline's discussion, citing Beekes, calls `ὁδός` uncertain and says the
  connection with “sit” is unclear. These are research curiosities, not chart
  candidates without a visibly disputed branch.
  https://www.etymonline.com/word/method · https://www.etymonline.com/word/synod · https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Reconstruction:Proto-Indo-European/sed-
- **recess, residue, surcease, cede** and the larger Latin `cēdere` family rely
  on the disputed proposal that `cēdō` continues a formation meaning “sit
  away.” The current Wiktionary root page marks that derivation as perhaps;
  they should not inflate the secure Latin `sedēre` family.
  https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Reconstruction:Proto-Indo-European/sed-
- **hostage** has a real Latin relative, `obses` “hostage,” built from `ob- +
  sedēre`; but deriving the English word *hostage* from that Latin noun competes
  with a `hospes` route. The Latin semantic parallel is interesting; the
  English descent is not secure enough for an undashed chart line.
  https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/obses · https://www.etymonline.com/word/hostage
