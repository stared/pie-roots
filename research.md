# The Star of *star* — research notes

Etymological data for the family of PIE **\*h₂stḗr 'star'**. Scope rules:

- The dataset covers the genuine \*h₂stḗr family. The homophonous roots (\*ster- 'stiff', \*sterh₃- 'spread', \*ster- 'barren') are documented in §5 as **non-members**.
- **English words are the focus**; non-English forms earn their place as ancestors of English words, plus a handful of headline cognates for breadth (one per branch).
- Drafted from Wiktionary + etymonline (URLs inline). **Cross-check status**: every English word audited against BOTH Wiktionary and etymonline (2026-07-08); diffed against Watkins/AHD ster-³ (verbatim) and Pokorny stē̆r-2 (full record); specialist-dictionary page cites confirmed where Wiktionary carries them (Beekes, Kroonen, Mayrhofer, Matasović, Martirosyan, NIL) — **open items in §7** (de Vaan and Kloekhorst pages). The full over-collected descendant pool is in the [Appendix](#appendix-full-wiktionary-descendant-superset-dump-2026-07-08).
- **Epistemic note.** Every statement here is a claim by fallible scholars, not a fact about prehistory; the sources also share ancestry (Wiktionary, etymonline, and AHD all lean on the same philological tradition), so their agreement is weaker evidence than independent confirmation would be. Status labels mean exactly this and no more: **solid** = no published disagreement found in the sources consulted; **disputed** = competing published derivations; **not a member** = a different derivation is positively established; **unknown** = cannot be traced (which is not evidence of unrelatedness). Reconstructions (\*-forms) are hypotheses by construction. Completeness is bounded by what was published and, before that, by what was ever written down.

## 1. The root: \*h₂stḗr

Per [Wiktionary's reconstruction page](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Reconstruction:Proto-Indo-European/h%E2%82%82st%E1%B8%97r):

- **Form**: masculine athematic **hysterokinetic r-stem** — nom.sg. \*h₂stḗr, gen.sg. \*h₂strés (oblique stem \*h₂str-), acc.sg. \*h₂stérm̥, nom.pl. \*h₂stéres. The full-grade vs. zero-grade alternation explains Greek ἀστήρ (full grade) vs. Sanskrit stṛ́ (zero grade, laryngeal lost).
- **Its own origin (disputed — see §4.3)**: "apparently" \*h₂e(h₁)s- 'to burn, glow' + agentive \*-tḗr — literally **"the glower, the shiner"** (cf. \*h₂éh₁tēr 'fire'). Attributed to Ringe (2006) and NIL (2008) via Wiktionary; hedged even there.
- **The laryngeal evidence**: Hittite ḫašterz(a) preserves ḫ- < \*h₂- — the standard argument that the word is inherited Indo-European (not a wandering loan).
- **Derived formations that head sub-trees**:
  - thematic \*h₂stér-o-m → Greek ἄστρον
  - l-diminutive (\*h₂stēr-leh₂ / Proto-Italic \*stērlā) → Latin stēlla
  - Germanic n-stem extension \*h₂stér-ōn → PGmc \*sternǭ / \*sterrô (Kroonen: \*sterr- by Kluge's law)
  - Proto-Anatolian \*Hstḗrs; Proto-Indo-Iranian \*Hstā́; Proto-Celtic ā-stem \*sterā; Proto-Tocharian \*ścär-iye-
- **The gap**: **Balto-Slavic has no inherited reflex.** Lithuanian žvaigždė, OCS zvězda, Polish gwiazda are from a different source — Pokorny reconstructs it as ĝhu̯oigʷ- 'to shine; star' (IEW 495); etymonline calls it "not explained" (s.v. [\*ster- (2)](https://www.etymonline.com/word/*ster-)).

## 4. Disputed links

### 4.1 sterling — status: **disputed** (link to steorra uncertain; a footnote, not a highlight)
Much-discussed, none of it decisive: OED prefers OE \*steorling 'little star' (stars on some Norman pennies — but etymonline notes "starred coins were not especially common"); Wiktionary carries OE \*stēre 'strong, stout' as the alternative; 'Easterling' is "considered long exploded"; Watkins/AHD doesn't list it under ster-³ at all. — https://www.etymonline.com/word/sterling, https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/sterling

### 4.2 Esther — status: **disputed** (two competing derivations, and they are not independent)
- **Persian 'star'**: from Old Persian star- (→ setāre), "a distant doublet of star" (Wiktionary); etymonline lists Esther under the star root. — https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Esther, https://www.etymonline.com/word/Esther
- **Ishtar**: "alternatively from Akkadian Ištar" (Wiktionary); Macchi (2019, via Wikipedia): probably Ishtar *or* the Persian star word — and the theories are not independent, since even the Persian route "implies an association with Ishtar."
- Minority: Persian 'myrtle'/'woman' proposals (mirroring her Hebrew name Hadassah 'myrtle').
- If via Ishtar and Ishtar is non-PIE, Esther drops out of the family; if via setāre, she's genuinely in. Both paths must be documented.

### 4.3 The root's own origin — status: **uncertain derivation; Semitic connection unresolved**

Two separate open questions stack at the root: (a) the internal derivation from \*h₂e(h₁)s- 'burn', hedged even on Wiktionary ("apparently"); (b) the Semitic connection, where even the direction of borrowing is contested.
- **'The glower'**: \*h₂e(h₁)s- 'burn, glow' + \*-tḗr (Ringe 2006, NIL 2008, via Wiktionary — hedged as "apparently").
- **The Semitic-loan question**: the old idea that PIE borrowed from Semitic (Ištar/ʕAṯtar) is doubted since Buck ("Buck and others doubt the old suggestion…" — etymonline). **Recent scholarship reverses the direction**: Wilson-Wright (2015, "A Star is Born") argues *Semitic borrowed from PIE* ("Speakers of common Semitic borrowed an early form of Indo-European \*H2ster- to denote the planet Venus," perhaps via Anatolian) — https://www.academia.edu/11490267/. Wiktionary's Proto-Semitic \*ʕaṯtar- page stays agnostic: "phonetic and semantic problems that are difficult to resolve" — https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Reconstruction:Proto-Semitic/%CA%95a%E1%B9%AFtar-
- The pop claim "English *star* comes from Ishtar" (e.g. Electrum Magazine 2018) is contradicted by mainstream references; the Hittite laryngeal is the key evidence for inheritance.

### 4.4 Greek ἀστραπή / στεροπή / ἀστεροπή 'lightning' — status: **contested membership**
- Traditional: 'star-eye' compound (\*h₂str- + h₃okʷ- 'eye'). Watkins/AHD keeps this, hedged: "possible compound … lightning, twinkling (< 'looking like a star'; ōps, op-, eye…)" — and accordingly lists English **astraphobia** under ster-³ (Watkins 2011, s.v. ster-³). Pokorny likewise groups the lightning words under stē̆r- with "wozu wohl" ('to which probably') (IEW 1027–1028).
- **Beekes: Pre-Greek substrate** — the variant shapes (ἀστραπή ~ στεροπή ~ ἀστεροπή) are "phonetically difficult to explain"; he follows Kuiper and Furnée in assigning Pre-Greek origin. — https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E1%BC%80%CF%83%CF%84%CF%81%CE%B1%CF%80%CE%AE
- Middle path (Scarborough 2023): a substrate word folk-etymologized *toward* ἀστήρ. Wiktionary's ἀστήρ entry notably does not list these as derivatives.
- Net: a genuine two-position dispute (Watkins/Pokorny: probably in; Beekes: out), not a settled exclusion.

### 4.5 Sanskrit tā́rā / Tara — status: **solid for the deity name; the given name is NOT from this root**
- stṛ́ → tṛ/tāra- is "a Vedic contraction" (Wiktionary, citing Mayrhofer EWAia I 643); presented as established fact. English **Tara** as the goddess/Buddhist deity name (doublet of sitara) continues it.
- **Correction (audit 2026-07-08)**: per Wiktionary, the common English **female given name Tara is from Irish Teamhair/Temair 'high place'** (the Hill of Tara, popularized via the *Gone with the Wind* plantation) — not from Sanskrit. Only the deity name belongs to the star family. — https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Tara#English
- Caveat on the deity name: within Sanskrit tradition Tārā is folk-etymologized as 'she who carries across' (√tṝ) — a homophony, not the etymology.

### 4.6 Latin stēlla — status: **solid**
- \*h₂stḗr → Proto-Italic \*stērlā (diminutive) → stēlla, with regular \*-rl- > -ll- (de Vaan 2008: 585, via https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Reconstruction:Proto-Italic/st%C4%93rl%C4%81). ## 5. Look-alike roots — NOT related to \*h₂stḗr

Three (!) other PIE roots sound like *star*, plus assorted red herrings. None is related to \*h₂stḗr. Etymonline lumps the first as [\*ster- (1) 'stiff'](https://www.etymonline.com/word/*ster-) vs. \*ster- (2) 'star'; Wiktionary splits 'stiff' into suffixed sub-roots.

### 5a. \*(s)ter- 'stiff, rigid'
**stare** (PGmc \*starāną 'be rigid' — staring is being *stiff-eyed*, not star-gazing), **stern** (adj., PGmc \*sturnijaz), **stark** (\*(s)terg- → \*starkaz), **starch** (same, 'stiffening'), **starve** (\*sterbʰ- 'become stiff' → PGmc \*sterbaną 'die' — cf. German sterben), **start** (\*sturtijan 'leap up'), **strut** (\*strūtōną 'be puffed up'), **redstart** ('red-tail', OE steort 'tail'), and — the surprise member — **stereo-** (Greek στερεός 'solid, firm'). URLs: Wiktionary s.vv. Disputed memberships: **stork** (etymonline: 'stiff-legged bird'; Wiktionary rejects, gives \*sr̥ǵos) and **torpid** (etymonline: here; de Vaan: separate \*terp-). Pure coincidence: **stertorous** (Latin stertere 'snore', imitative).

### 5b. \*sterh₃- 'to spread, strew'
Via Latin sternō/strātus: **street** (< via strāta 'paved road' — a very early Latin loan into Germanic), **stratum, stratify, stratosphere, substrate, prostrate, consternation** (⚠ the 'dismay' verb is only indirectly tied to 'spread'). Via Greek στρατός 'army' (a "spread-out host" < \*str̥h₃tós): **strategy, stratagem**. Via the extended form \*strew-: **strew, straw, strawberry**; and (⚠ Wiktionary's position, not consensus) Latin struō 'pile up, build' → **structure, construct, destroy, instrument**. URLs: https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Reconstruction:Proto-Indo-European/sterh%E2%82%83-, .../strew-.

### 5c. \*ster- 'barren' and the steering herrings
**sterile** (Latin sterilis, Greek στεῖρος) < a third homophone \*ster(h₁)- 'barren' — https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Reconstruction:Proto-Indo-European/ster-. **steer, stern** (of a ship, < ON stjórn 'steering'), **starboard** (OE stēor 'rudder' + bord) — the steering family, no star. **starling** — OE stærlinc, stær + -ling, its own bird-word root (Pokorny storos/stornos, IEW 1036 — which also gives German Star 'starling'); unrelated to both star and sterling. **astound/astonish** — Latin ex-+tonāre 'thunder', not astrum. **consider, desire** — Latin sīdus 'constellation': star-adjacent in *meaning* but a different lexeme entirely (Latin had two star words, and English inherited derivatives of both). **hysteria** — ὑστέρα 'womb'. **sitar** — Persian seh 'three' + tār 'string' (tār ≠ setāre; caution: Wiktionary's ستاره descendant list shows Bengali সেতার in a way that invites conflation — do not merge them). **Zoroaster** — Avestan zaraθuštra; the -aster resemblance is at most Greek folk-etymology. **poetaster, criticaster** — the pejorative suffix -aster is a separate Latin diminutive suffix, not the star word (the star-derived -aster exists only as a Translingual taxonomic suffix for starfish-like genera — https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/-aster). **satire** — Latin satura '(fruit-)full dish, medley' < satur 'sated' < PIE \*seh₂- 'satisfy'; its association with Greek satyrs is itself a Latin folk etymology — no star and no aster — https://www.etymonline.com/word/satire. **east, Easter** — OE ēast < PGmc \*aust- 'toward the sunrise' < PIE \*h₂ews- 'to shine (of dawn)' (also aurora, Eos; Easter via the dawn goddess Ēostre) — a *different* sky-glow root, semantically next door to the star word's possible parent \*h₂e(h₁)s- 'burn' but distinct; the viral "Easter < Ishtar" claim is the same pop-etymology error as "star < Ishtar" (§4.3) — https://www.etymonline.com/word/east.

A separate status, distinct from the demonstrated non-members above — **origin unknown** (a lack of proof is not proof of lack; these can be neither claimed nor debunked):
- **tiara** — Latin tiāra < Greek τιάρα (a Persian headdress), "of unknown ultimate origin" per Wiktionary. No source connects it to the star word — but none rules it out either; it simply cannot be traced past Greek. — https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/tiara
- (Same epistemic class as Balto-Slavic zvezda's source and Proto-Semitic \*ʕaṯtar- — "unknown" is its own category in this dataset, not a synonym for "unrelated.")
